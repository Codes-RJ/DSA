# Recreational Problems

## Introduction
Recreational problems are "just for fun" but often reveal sophisticated algorithmic concepts.

## 1. N-Queens (Revisited)
While an interview classic, the N-Queens problem is a staple of recreational mathematics.

## 2. Knight's Tour
**Question**: Can a knight visit every square on a chessboard exactly once?
- **Solution**: Warnsdorff's rule (move to the square with the fewest onward moves) is a successful heuristic.

## 3. Stable Marriage Problem
**Question**: Matching two sets of people such that no two people would prefer each other over their current partner.
- **Solution**: Gale-Shapley Algorithm. Used in actual residency matching for doctors and organ donation.

## 4. Magic Squares
**Question**: An NxN grid where every row, column, and diagonal sums to the same constant.
- **Magic Constant**: `M = n(n² + 1) / 2`.

## 5. Sudoku, Crosswords, and Nonograms
Solving these puzzles via **Constraint Satisfaction Problems (CSP)** and backtracking algorithms.

## Why study recreational puzzles?
- **Creativity**: They often require "outside the box" thinking.
- **Graph Theory**: Many map directly to graph problems (Hamiltonian path for Knight's tour).
- **Optimization**: Finding the *best* way to solve a game or puzzle.
- **Heuristic Development**: Good practice for designing rules of thumb that work for complex problems.

## Summary
Recreational problems keep your problem-solving skills sharp and offer a fun way to apply advanced data structures and algorithms in a tangible way.
